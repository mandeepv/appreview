For generations, parents were told that discipline, obedience, and control were the foundations of raising good children.

And for a long time, this was considered "common sense."

But over the last few decades, research in neuroscience, psychology, and child development has quietly overturned many of these assumptions.

• Fear, punishment, or authority teach right from wrong

Traditional methods often appear effective in the short term.

When a child is overwhelmed, scared, or emotionally flooded:

• The thinking part of the brain goes offline

• The survival part takes over

## Screen 6

Modern parenting focuses on helping children develop:

These skills grow through co-regulation, not force.

• Emotional regulation

• Internal discipline

• Empathy

# Lesson 2

## Screen 1

**Happiness Chemicals**

Most people assume happiness comes from getting more of what feels good — but research shows that misunderstanding how happiness works can reduce long-term well-being.

In this lesson, you'll learn how three key body chemicals shape short-term and long-term happiness.

Why does trying to make ourselves happy often backfire?


## Screen 2

**Why Happiness Backfires**

When people try to increase happiness, they usually focus on the wrong chemical.

They chase short-term pleasure, novelty, or rewards — believing these will create lasting happiness.

But this strategy often leaves people feeling:

To understand why, we need to look inside the brain.

• Unsatisfied

• Restless

• Emotionally drained


## Screen 3

**The Three Happiness Chemicals**

Your sense of happiness is influenced mainly by three chemicals:

Most people focus almost entirely on one of these — often without realizing it.

• Dopamine → short-term reward & motivation

• Oxytocin → connection & bonding

• Cortisol → stress & survival


## Screen 4

**The Chemical Most People Chase**

Most people are subconsciously trying to increase dopamine.

Dopamine is released when we:

• See something new or exciting

• Accomplish a task

• Eat something pleasurable

• Scroll social media

It feels like happiness — but it isn't designed to last.


## Screen 5

**What Dopamine Really Does**

Dopamine is a short-term reward system.

It gives you a brief boost of pleasure or motivation after you do something — like:

• Finishing a task

• Feeding the kids

• Taking a shower

• Buying something new

Its job is to encourage action — not to create lasting happiness.


## Screen 6

**Dopamine Always Fades**

Dopamine is designed to go away quickly, no matter what caused it.

If dopamine stayed high, the brain would stop motivating you to act.

So the brain always pulls you back to baseline.

That's not a flaw — it's a feature.


## Screen 7

**Why New Things Don't Make Us Happy for Long**

Research shows:

• A favorite food → dopamine spike for ~10 minutes

• A new car → a few minutes of extra happiness per day, for a month or two

• A new house → slightly increased happiness for a few months

For 99% of purchases, any happiness boost lasts less than a few hours.


## Screen 8

**The Happiness Illusion**

Even though dopamine fades quickly, we keep believing:

So people:

• Envy others

• Chase upgrades

• Look for the next hit

> "If I had what they have, I'd be happier."

But the brain always returns to baseline.


## Screen 9

**Why Chasing Dopamine Backfires**

Trying to maximize dopamine leads to:

No matter how much you pour in, it keeps draining out.

• Constant craving

• Short-lived satisfaction

• Lower long-term happiness

It's like trying to keep a leaky bucket full of water.


## Screen 10

**Dopamine Isn't "Bad"**

Dopamine isn't the enemy.

You need dopamine to:

It was never designed for that job.

• Get things done

• Stay motivated

• Enjoy small wins

The problem is expecting dopamine to provide long-term happiness.


## Screen 11

**So What Should We Focus On?**

If dopamine isn't the key to lasting happiness…

There are other chemicals that play a much bigger role in:

We'll explore those next.

• Emotional stability

• Connection

• Long-term well-being

What does matter?


## Screen 12

**BEFORE THE QUIZ**

**What You've Learned So Far**

• Dopamine is a short-term reward chemical

• It always fades, no matter the source

• Chasing dopamine lowers long-term happiness

• Lasting well-being requires different systems

Now let's lock this in.

# Lesson 3

## Screen 1

**DAY 4 · FOUNDATIONS**

**The Long-Term Unhappiness Chemical**

Some chemicals give short bursts of pleasure.

Others quietly shape who we become over years.

This lesson is about cortisol — the chemical most linked to long-term unhappiness when it stays high for too long.


## Screen 2

**Cortisol Isn't the Enemy**

Cortisol is a stress hormone.

A small amount is healthy and necessary:

• It helps us wake up

• It motivates action

• It prepares us for challenges

The problem isn't cortisol itself — it's chronic cortisol.


## Screen 3

**The Real Problem: Constant Alert**

High cortisol in children usually doesn't come from one big traumatic event.

This state is far more damaging than occasional stress.

It comes from being on edge — from feeling that something emotionally upsetting might happen at any moment.


## Screen 4

**Where High Cortisol Often Comes From**

This may be uncomfortable to hear:

Not through abuse — but through unpredictability.

Examples include:

• Sudden anger

• Sarcasm or teasing

• Comparisons to other children

• Emotional withdrawal

High cortisol in children is most often caused unintentionally by parents.


## Screen 5

**KEY INSIGHT**

**It's Not the Event — It's the Anticipation**

It's not the criticism itself that raises cortisol most.

Even if nothing happens today, the child's nervous system stays on high alert.

It's the fear that it could happen again.

This is how cortisol stays elevated without you realizing it.


## Screen 6

**Why You Might Not Notice It**

Many children with high cortisol:

The effects appear months — or years — later.

• Behave well

• Perform fine in school

• Show no obvious problems

Cortisol damage is often delayed.


## Screen 7

**What Research Shows**

Hundreds of studies on children's cortisol levels show that consistently high cortisol is linked to:

A study in Child Development also found links to:

• Substance abuse

• Poor life decisions

• Criminal behavior

• Lower long-term happiness

• Learning deficits

• Cognitive delays


## Screen 8

**Why Cortisol Is Like Smoking**

One exposure doesn't hurt. Neither does a week. Sometimes not even months.

But over time, it significantly damages:

• Emotional regulation

• Mental health

• Decision-making

High cortisol is like smoking.


## Screen 9

**What Chronic Cortisol Does to a Person**

People with chronically high cortisol are more likely to feel:

• Anxious

• Depressed

• Angry

• Easily triggered

This isn't a personality flaw — it's a nervous system stuck in survival mode.


## Screen 10

**IMPORTANT**

**This Is Not About Blame**

Most parents increase cortisol without knowing it.

Even calm, loving parents can do this unintentionally.

The good news: Cortisol is highly responsive to changes in the environment.


## Screen 11

**Cortisol Can Be Reduced**

Research shows that when parents:

Children's cortisol levels can drop — and some negative effects can even be reversed.

• Learn to spot cortisol triggers

• Reduce emotional unpredictability

You don't need to be perfect. You need to be predictably safe.


## Screen 12

**BEFORE THE QUIZ**

**What Comes Next**

Now that we understand what to reduce…

But first, let's lock this in.

The next lesson focuses on what to increase — the chemical that builds long-term emotional safety and connection.

How does it actually help?


### Screen 5

**The Science**

When we give something such as a difficult emotion a name or a label, it allows our logical brain to help regulate and organize the primitive and emotional parts of our brain.

When our feelings are swirling around us in an unorganized chaos, the logical brain can't help.

It can only help if it understands.


### Screen 6

**Research Proof**

By giving the emotion a name, the logical brain can step in and calm the other parts down.

UCLA BRAIN MAPPING CENTER

— Matthew Lieberman, UCLA

# Lesson 11: Dissociation

## Sub-lesson 1

### Screen 1

**Your brain has a hidden escape button**

Your brain has a fascinating trick to get you out of uncomfortable situations.

It usually happens at least once a week — and most of the time, you don't even realize it happened.

Sometimes this protects us. Other times, it quietly hurts our relationships.


### Screen 2

**What is dissociation?**

Dissociation (also called avoidance) is what happens when something feels too overwhelming for your brain to process.

Instead of thinking through it, your brain briefly checks out.

You suddenly go blank

You stop thinking about the issue

You mentally turn to something else


### Screen 3

**What does dissociation feel like?**

Dissociation happens instantly.

"My mind just went blank"

"I don't know what to say"

"I don't see a solution"

"I just want to think about something else"

This usually happens without conscious choice.


### Screen 4

**A simple example**

Imagine this:

Your child can only attend one school. Someone says to you:

"I've heard that school isn't as good as it used to be."

Option A — No dissociation

"Maybe I should talk to the principal."

"Let me see if there's a solution."

Option B — Dissociation

"There's nothing we can do."

"I don't want to think about this."

(Mind goes blank)

Key insight

Dissociation happens when your brain decides there is no solution, so it shuts the problem down.


### Screen 5

**Question:** What is dissociation?

a) When you decide not to associate with a group

b) What happens when someone says something that overwhelms you [CORRECT]

c) The breaking down of memories inside your brain

*Feedback: Exactly. Dissociation is a mental shutdown, not a choice.*


### Screen 6

**Question:** True or False: Dissociation happens frequently and we are usually unaware that it occurred.

a) True [CORRECT]

b) False

*Feedback: Correct. Most dissociation happens below awareness, which is why it can quietly affect communication.*


### Screen 7

**Awareness is the first step**

In the next sublesson, you'll learn how to notice dissociation in real time — and what to do instead.


## Sub-lesson 2

### Screen 1

**How do we not dissociate?**

Dissociating — or avoiding — feels helpful in the moment, but it usually makes things worse.

Avoidance makes us feel helpless, even though situations are almost never as hopeless as they seem.

When something feels overwhelming, the healthier response is not to push it away — but to say:

"This is a hard problem. I can't think about it right now, but I can come back to it once I've calmed down."

When our logical brain regains control, we are usually able to face the problem and find solutions.


### Screen 2

**What happens when we don't avoid?**

•

Let's take an example.

Imagine you're worried about your child's school. If you don't dissociate, you might:

Look up research

Talk to teachers

Meet the principal

Explore specific solutions

You may learn — as many studies have shown — that teachers matter more than the school itself.

That opens up options.

Avoiding the thought feels easier, but facing it usually gives you more control, not less.


### Screen 3

**What if I just dissociate?**

When you dissociate, the problem doesn't go away.

It keeps nagging you in the background.

In that school example, you may start feeling:

Like you're failing as a parent

Like you should be doing more

Like something is unresolved

That emotional weight builds up — quietly.

People dissociate a lot:

When facts challenge their beliefs

When truths feel uncomfortable

When something feels unsolvable

Once you start noticing it, you'll see it everywhere — in yourself and others.


### Screen 4

**The solution is simpler than it sounds**

The good news is this:

You don't need to solve the problem immediately.

Simply noticing dissociation when it happens and giving it a name already reduces its power.

You might say to yourself:

"I'm dissociating right now. I'm overwhelmed and feel stuck. I'll come back to this tomorrow when I can think clearly."

Naming it creates space.

Space creates options.

That's how we see more solutions and make better choices — for ourselves and our loved ones.


### Screen 5

**Question:** Why is dissociating or avoidance usually a bad reaction?

a) It makes us feel helpless

b) It prevents us from working on solutions

*Feedback: Correct. Dissociation creates helplessness, blocks solutions, and leaves emotional weight unresolved — making things worse over time.*


### Screen 6

c) Notice it and name it [CORRECT]

d) Distract yourself

*Feedback: Correct. Naming dissociation creates awareness and space, which helps you regain control and see more options.*


## Sub-lesson 3

### Screen 1

Why this helps

This reconnects your brain directly to your surroundings.


### Screen 6

Cold is a fast and effective grounding tool.

Run cold water over your wrists

Use only what feels safe and manageable for you.
